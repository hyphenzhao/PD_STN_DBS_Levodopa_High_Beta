# Dissociable Modulation of Subthalamic Beta Sub-bands and Intermuscular Coherence by Levodopa and Deep Brain Stimulation Suggests a Physiological Role of High-beta Bursts in Gait Coordination
## Abstract
Objective: To contrast how levodopa and STN-DBS differentially shape low- and high-beta activity in the subthalamic nucleus (STN) and their impact on lower-limb muscle cooperation during different posture and free walking in Parkinson’s disease (PD).
Methods: In 28 people with Parkinson’s disease (53 STN hemispheres), we recorded bilateral STN local field potentials (LFP) and EMGs from bilateral antagonistic lower-limb muscle pairs during sitting, standing, and free walking. A subset (n=10, 20 hemispheres) was additionally studied OFF and ON unilateral high-frequency STN-DBS with LFPs recorded from the contralateral (non-stimulated) STN. We investigated how levodopa and DBS modulated beta-band power, beta burst metrics, intermuscular coherence (IMC), and gait-cycle phase–locked modulation.
Results: Levodopa selectively reduced low-beta STN power and burst occupancy (p<0.001), with limited effect in the high-beta change. By contrast, continuous STN-DBS significantly shortened burst occupancy in both sub-beta-bands (p<0.001) despite minimal effects on relative beta power. Across conditions, high-beta burst occupancy in the STN LFPs positively correlated with lower-limb IMC and was associated with better posture/gait scores. During free walking, levodopa reduced low-beta bursts while increasing gait-phase related modulation in the high-beta band but with no effect on lower-limb IMC.  In comparison, DBS broadly curtailed bursts around stance–swing transitions and reduced IMC during stance-phase of free walking.
Conclusion: Medication and stimulation have distinct effects on beta sub-band signatures. Levodopa primarily suppresses low-beta activity in the STN, whereas continuous DBS additionally reduces high-beta bursts, which correlates with decreased IMC. Levodopa also increased the gait-phase related modulation in the beta band, whereas DBS did not have such effect. In addition, DBS, but not levodopa, reduced lower-limb IMC during free walking. These results may explain potential negative effects of continuous high frequency DBS on gait. These findings also support adaptive DBS strategies that selectively suppress prolonged low-beta while sparing brief, gait-phase–specific high-beta bursts to preserve physiological muscle cooperation.
## Highlights
•	Levodopa selectively reduced STN low-beta power and burst occupancy with little change in high-beta, whereas continuous STN-DBS reduced beta burst occupancy in both low- and high-beta.
•	High-beta burst occupancy correlated with greater intermuscular coherence in lower-limb antagonist muscle pairs (tibialis–gastrocnemius) and with reduced posture and gait impairment, suggesting a potential beneficial role of high-beta activity in normal posture and gait control.
•	During free walking, levodopa reduced low-beta activity while increasing gait-phase modulation in both low-beta high-beta. In contrast, DBS suppressed broad-band beta, lowered stance-phase intermuscular coherence, and blunted gait-phase modulation, supporting sub beta band- and gait phase-targeted adaptive DBS strategies.
